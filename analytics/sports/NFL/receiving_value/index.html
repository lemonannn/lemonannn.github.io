Predicting NFL Draft Slot with Production For College Recievers
A few days ago, I did something that I think everyone in the analytical side of the draft scouting process has done before. I wanted to create a model to try to determine how high a player should be taken in the draft based on their statistics. Over the past couple weeks, I’ve been studying the wide receiver group in the 2026 draft with both their numbers and their film, but I wanted to see how their college statistics actually stacked up against receivers in previous classes. For me, creating a model like this is always about combining the best modeling techniques with a theoretical groundwork that takes ideas from my actual football knowledge. For this project, I started by asking myself what kind of numbers from college receivers generally translates to a better NFL prospect. A key word that I focused on throughout my analysis was production, that is to say, rather than focusing on every little detail in a player’s profile that might be relevant to where they’re drafted, I wanted to focus on how actual in-game impact at the college level specifically translated to a player’s draft slot. To do this, I identified several traits in a receiver that likely lead to a generally higher drafted receiver. First, efficiency, or how effective a receiver is considering the opportunities they receive. Next, pure production, a receiver’s overall contribution, in terms of what can be gathered from their actual statistics, on the field. Third, blocking, or how much value they contribute to the run game. Last, positional value is also a factor, as getting a solid slot receiver is easier than getting an average outside wideout. The last clear focus I wanted to accomplish with my model was to ensure that a player’s entire time in college was an influence, rather than just their senior year. There are plenty of players that don’t live up to their senior year expectations for one reason or another, and that’s another topic I have a good interest in studying, but especially in that case it seems important to consider their entire body of work. That’s not to say we should completely ignore a player’s worse years. In the end, it’s all data that we have to contextualize and understand to get a better idea of the player’s profile. The designed model measured three out of the four listed production traits that were desirable to measure, with blocking being the major exception. Finding a reliable blocking evaluation for a player, especially a college receiver, is unreliable at best. While unable to measure blocking effectively, there’s great metrics for efficiency, pure production, and positional value that serve as representatives for these assets. For positional value, slot percentage is a good indicator of where a player would be utilized in the NFL. Generally, a player who plays in the slot in college is going to be there at the next level and a receiver that plays on the outside in college is going to do so in the NFL as well, though the latter is less reliable. The best way to measure efficiency is yards per route run. Overall, this is a good metric, but it does struggle to capture the overall idea of ‘opportunity’, as we defined efficiency. Each route run by a player isn’t equivalent in opportunity. For example, a play where a receiver is the intended target is not equal in opportunity to a play where he runs what is essentially a clear-out route. However, Y/RR is the statistic that best captures this idea, even if it doesn’t include these nuances. It’s simply something that has to be included in understanding the results of the model, rather than part of the model itself. This is a great example of the lack of data we have in evaluating college receivers, and in this case NFL receivers as well. What is able to be tracked isn’t enough to capture even the simplest ideas about a player, where it’s vital to marry statistics with film and scheme. Finding a good statistic to use for production is a more interesting task, with consideration of the variable we’re trying to predict. There’s two potential values we could try to predict with our model. First, how successful a player was in the NFL. This is an obvious metric and would allow us to use the model for predicting NFL success in future receivers. However, this idea comes with one major problem: How do we actually measure success in a receiver? This is another question that could warrant another project altogether (and probably will. Stay tuned!), but the easy answer is using a metric like Football Reference Approximate Value. However, this value is imprecise and would have to be averaged to include a fair comparison for younger players. Plus, it’d probably necessitate leaving out at least the most recent draft classes due to a lack of critical data, which is something that I really preferred not doing due to the already small number of data points. So, rather than predicting NFL success, I decided to predict when a player would be drafted, with a receiver’s draft slot as the variable being predicted in the model. So, what metrics that correspond with efficiency and production will be the best predictors of when a player is picked. After considering a few different options, one of which included splitting a receiver’s statistics by the kind of coverage they faced, I decided on splitting a receiver’s efficiency and pure production by the depth of any given reception. This splits receptions by where the ball was caught, whether it was behind the line of scrimmage, within ten yards, between ten and twenty yards out, or deeper than twenty yards out. The thought process behind this decision was fairly simple. Basically, certain kinds of production are more translatable to production in the NFL. As cornerbacks get better, it’s disproportionally more important to be able to beat them with high end traits, which means that certain production profiles are drafted higher than others, even if their total production is the same. A receiver that’s able to consistently win deep is generally going to be drafted higher than a receiver that does all of his work in the short game, because that kind of production usually translates better to the NFL. The next question to answer was what kind of model was best. I considered a simple GAM, but that kind of model doesn’t support the complex interaction between variables that I wanted to account for. An XGBoost model was the next potential answer, but that came with an opposite problem: it came with too many interaction terms that would inevitably result in overfitting with such a small sample size. With a firm outline in mind for the foundation of the model, the best option was using a neural network. Like any of these options, it comes with the big asterisk that there’s no way to know exactly what’s happening within the model aside from testing what kind of inputs result in a specific projected slot, but it’s the best way to capture the exact interactions that are necessary to evaluate players based on their depth splits. To better ensure that the model decreases the predicted draft slot as production increases, an option is to make each function monotonous. However, with as few data points as are available, constraining the model in such a way would decrease the effectiveness to the point that it’s just not worth it. Therefore, there’s no guarantee that the model is completely monotonous, but the sample size is large enough that it should be close to monotonous, and therefore effective in the intended purposes. The model design was based on the described fundamentals, with pure production and efficiency being balanced within the neural network to determine an overall score. Then, that value was increased if the prospect went to a power four school. Ideally, you’d scope this down into tiers of schools based on how difficult it is to produce there, but determining those tiers would be largely subject to error and year by year change in scheme and talent. Then, a baseline of drop rate and positional value is added to account for those factors, reaching a final production score indicated by a predicted draft slot. The created model was off by approximately 56 picks on average, which is almost two rounds. Obviously, this value is very large, especially considering that there’s only about 260 picks in the draft, meaning that a completely blind model would be around 65 picks off on average. This shows a very poor performance from the model, but a performance that is clearly better than randomly guessing. Looking at the predicted decrease in draft slot at Y/RR increases, and it’s exactly as hoped. There’s a clear negative correlation with clear scattering due to differences in pure production and drop/wide rates. So, why are the predictions so inaccurate overall? The first clear indication when assessing the model is that it’s relatively unconfident about predicting a player’s slot. The lowest predicted slot of all players was that of Elijah Moore, who was predicted to go approximately 31st overall. This came from his 2020 season, where he put up ridiculously efficient numbers in a year in which he dealt with injuries. This was almost exactly his actual draft slot (he went 34th), but it also shows that high end production, even in the power four, doesn’t always result in a top draft prospect. Another notable example of this is Malik Washington at Virginia, who had top ten percentile production that predicted he’d go at approximately 67th overall, but didn’t go until 184th overall. These are two players that thrived in their college schemes, both as the top option. However, particularly in Washington’s case, his production just didn’t project to translate to NFL production. Looking at the other side of things, Malik Nabers had a similar predicted slot to Malik Washington, but he was a clear top ten pick. The difference between the two, which largely stems from the massive physical difference between them, is that Naber’s production cleanly translates to NFL production, while teams were concerned Washington’s production wouldn’t translate at all against better defenses. For an even more extreme example, we can look at Michael Thomas, who had bottom 25 percentile production at Ohio State. This projects him at approximately pick 141, when in reality he went 47th overall and quickly became a great player. These are just a few examples in a sea of similar differences between projected slots and where they were actually drafted, but it shows a couple major influences on what is causing the model to be so inaccurate. First, and of smaller concern, is player size. The model accounts for slot value, which should somewhat penalize players with undersized frames, but the reality is that it’s very difficult for undersized receivers like Washington to provide value at the NFL level. On the other side of things, players with ideal size like Thomas are more primed to do so. This could be included in the model, and it would certainly increase its accuracy, but extracting conclusions like this is exactly why the model was limited to production related values in the first place. There are plenty of ways to create a more accurate model, but none of them are truly accurate enough to reliably suggest a draft position for a player without bringing interpretation of film or scheme into the mix. The more notable flaw in the production model is that it is situation blind, bringing back the film or scheme discussion that ended the previous paragraph. The bottom line is that production is completely subject to usage within an offense. Every college football team runs a different scheme that fluctuates from year to year based on the players within, making it extremely difficult, and probably truly impossible, to account for this using just data. At the very least, it’s impossible given the current scope of how football is played and tracked, both for fans and for data scientists within the NFL. Virginia’s scheme in 2023 was completely built around having Malik Washington flourish and dominate. Say, if he had been at Ohio State alongside MHJ, Emeka Egbuka, and a young Carnell Tate, his numbers would look completely different. The high talent level at Ohio State and the offensive scheme there also forces receivers into a similar role that they’d be in at the NFL level, giving them a preparation advantage compared to smaller school prospects. Basically, when considering production, it’s always important to consider the environment of that production in order to better evaluate if it’ll translate to an NFL role. Throughout this entire thing, the translation of production to the NFL has been a clear theme. The bottom line is, if a player is able to produce at a similar level in the NFL as they did in college, they’ll probably be a very good player. Six-hundred yards across a thirteen game season translates to approximately 785 yards in a seventeen game NFL season, which would suggest a valuable starter. Then, the question becomes how we know if a player’s production will translate well. Seeing that there’s plenty of highly drafted receivers that don’t end up justifying their draft slot, it seems like there’s no clear answer to this translation question. When looking at this problem from an analytical standpoint, the optimal solution is to draft players with few analytical red flags. Players with more complete profiles, say, a 6’2, 200 pound receiver that runs a 4.4 forty, produces on all levels of the field and against both zone and man coverage, has a great passer rating when targeted, has a high contested catch percentage but doesn’t rely on contested catches to be valuable, doesn’t drop the ball, and is incredibly efficient in his routes, is extremely likely to be successful. Then, consider the film viewer’s perspective. When looking at a receiver, it’s about analyzing their sharpness in routes, ability to win downfield, feel for coverage before and after the catch, ability and willingness to block, what kind of role the receiver would work best at in any given team’s offense, attention to detail, smoothness in working across the field, physicality at the top of the route and at the catch point, and comfortability in catching the ball, among countless other traits that all come together to paint a bigger picture of the receiver as a whole. If done perfectly, it’ll pinpoint exactly what a player is currently and how they’ll develop over the course of their career. However, it’s extremely difficult to properly assess receivers against college level competition and in offenses that are sometimes in different stratospheres when compared to any given NFL offense. In order to create the best scouting process imaginable, it’s important to marry the data and the film. Running an NFL draft scouting department utilizing just machine learning, as some seem to suggest could be the future, isn’t just unfeasible. It’s negligent, and goes against the theory of analytics in the first place. The problem isn’t the models themselves, as they’ve clearly been effective in other industries (healthcare, business, etc.), but with the data itself. College football data as it exists today is almost totally unaware, with so much of a player’s production depending on the environment around them. Schemes, especially in college where the year over year change in personnel is immense, fluctuates so much that even data from the same player on the same team one year apart can have a completely different analytical profile. When compounded with multiple teams and every single nuanced position across multiple seasons, it results in a dataset that isn’t strong enough to create truly accurate models for projecting players to the next level. Just because these models aren’t accurate enough to work on their own, though, doesn’t mean that they’re not helpful. The analytical profile can help scouts contextualize what they see on film, and vice-versa. I’ve heard people say “the film lies” or “the analytics lie”, but in reality they’re the most trustworthy resources in football. They spell exactly what a player was able to contribute on the field. Nothing less, and nothing more. As a statistician, success is all (a lot of the time) about minimizing loss, and to do that it’s vital that the data coming into the analysis holds as much information as possible about what’s being analyzed. In football, that’s about combining what you see on the field with what the statistics tell you, and often nuances such as the character of the player as well. In this discussion, it’s important to think about what kind of data could advance the analytical side of the game. Player tracking data is the obvious answer, eliminating problems regarding too little data by looking at the game at a microscopic level. This would introduce an all new kind of data that’s more responsive to machine learning techniques, but it still doesn’t account for many of the nuanced traits that inform player evaluation. The other thing to consider is that data gathering in football is a very recent trend, and most of the useful data comes in the last ten or so years. This is compounded by how significantly the game has changed in recent memory, largely due to analytics, and there’s no guarantee that the game won’t continue to shift moving forward. If anything, movements in 2025, like the heavy shift towards utilizing a nickel corner and some teams’ increased usage of 12 and 13 personnel, signify that the game will continue to adapt at a high rate. Even so, as time goes on the available data will naturally become larger and more specific, meaning that teams can put a higher weight on these kinds of models. Still, a future where an organization can completely rely on machine learning to build a team is an unlikely one. There’s always going to be details in film that would be lost in translation to statistics. That’s part of the beauty of the game of football and the world of analytics within the sport. It’s a game defined by its different schemes and player utilization, a constant marriage of scouting and analytics, understanding how to draft the best players and put them in the best role to contribute. The complexities of football are what make it so compelling not only as an analyst but also as a fan. Every little detail matters, and they’re not going to stop mattering anytime soon. In the current landscape of football, a team can’t just understand one side or the other. To be the best, to win a Super Bowl, scouting and analytics have to work as one.
